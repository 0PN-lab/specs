## Executive summary

**Problem**: Digital identification enables controllers to infer identifiers instantly across borders before an individual can verify who is collecting data.

**Solution**: This profile inverts the architecture by putting **Controller-ID first, not Individual-ID first**. Controllers publish a **Controller Identification Record (CIR)** at a public location before any processing begins.

**Key innovation**: Bilateral receipts and Notice Event Logs enable proof of transparency state and enable scaled, automated oversight.

# Part 1 ‚Äî Co-regulated notice receipt architecture

## 1. Scope

### 1.1 Profile objectives

**Primary innovation: Four-stage ANCR (Anchored Notice and Consent Receipt) authorization exchange**

- **Stage 1: Notice Receipt** (mandatory)
    - Controller presents CIR before requesting any PII.
    - Individual can remain anonymous.
    - Both parties hold synchronized proof (Two-Factor Notice).
- **Stage 2: Authorization Receipt** (optional; context-dependent)
    - Individual returns Stage 1 receipt with explicit authorization.
- **Stage 3: Micro Notice Credential** (optional)
    - Cryptographically verifiable credential for API/device authorization.
- **Stage 4: Notice Token** (optional)
    - Portable authorization token anchored to individual-controlled identifier (for cross-controller portability).

### 1.2 Scope: digital privacy across all legal bases

Applies to PII processing under any Convention 108+ lawful basis:

- Consent
- Contract
- Legal obligation
- Legitimate interest
- Vital interest
- Public interest

### 1.3 Profile boundaries

- Stage 1 (Notice Receipt) is mandatory for base conformance.
- Stages 2 to 4 are optional protocol extensions.
- Field-level compatibility maintained with ISO/IEC 27560-1:2025 Section 6.5.

### 1.4 Terminology: notice and consent receipts

- **Notice Receipt**: universal bilateral record documenting controller notification under any legal basis.
- **Privacy receipt** (base standard term): notice receipt in PII contexts.
- **Consent receipt** (TS 27560:2023 term): notice receipt with authorization_type = consent_granted.

## 6. ANCR Exchange Stage 1 ‚Äî field specification

**Objective**: Specify the **Stage 1 (Notice Receipt)** field requirements for the Universal Notice Receipt Profile, implementing **Controller-ID first** and **anonymous-by-default** issuance.

### 6.1 Relationship to base ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023 clause 6.3 structure

Stage 1 SHALL remain field-compatible with the base six-section organization described in ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023 clause 6.3.2.2:

- record header section
- PII processing
- event
- purposes
- PII information section
- party identification section

Stage 1 implements the same sections, with the profile-specific architectural inversion that **pii_principal_id SHALL NOT be present** at Stage 1.

### 6.2 Stage 1 core architectural requirements

Stage 1 implementations SHALL:

1. Provide a Notice Receipt that can be issued and verified **without any PII Principal identification**.
2. Bind the Notice Receipt to a verifiable **Controller Identification Record (CIR)**.
3. Support **Two-Factor Notice (2FN)** so both the controller and the individual can hold synchronized proof-of-notice.
4. Provide a queryable, append-only **Notice Event Log** reference for state changes.

### 6.3 Stage 1 field specification (base mapping + profile extensions)

The Stage 1 field specification is defined as:

- **Base-field compatibility** with ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023 (same field names and section placement), and
- **Profile extensions** required to support controller-id-first, notice-first issuance.

The complete Stage 1 mapping and specification is maintained in the workspace as:

- [Section 6.3: MVCR Field Mapping to Base ISO/IEC 27560:2023](https://www.notion.so/Section-6-3-MVCR-Field-Mapping-to-Base-ISO-IEC-27560-2023-e06aeb9017f643cdb006253435c81202?pvs=21)

For convenience, the Stage 1 specification requirements are summarized below as *mandatory Stage 1 requirements*.

#### 6.3.1 Record header section (Stage 1)

Stage 1 SHALL include:

- `schema_version`
- `receipt_id` (base record_id renamed for receipt instance tracking)
- `receipt_timestamp` (base record_timestamp renamed)
- `controller_identification_record_id` (CIR-ID) **(profile-required)**
- `notice_receipt_type` = "Stage 1" **(profile-required)**
- `two_factor_notice_indicator` = true **(profile-required)**

Stage 1 SHALL NOT include:

- `pii_principal_id`

#### 6.3.2 PII processing section (Stage 1)

Stage 1 SHALL include the base processing disclosure fields applicable to notice, including:

- `language`
- `lawful_basis`
- `pii_information`
- `pii_controllers`
- `withdrawal_method` (where consent may apply later)

Stage 1 SHALL additionally include:

- `scope_of_disclosure` **(profile-required)**

Stage 1 MAY include:

- `permissions_bundle` (requested granular permissions; becomes mandatory at Stage 2+ when granted)

#### 6.3.3 Party identification section (Stage 1)

Stage 1 SHALL include controller party identification using base party fields (unchanged), structured as a Controller Identification Record (CIR) scoped to `party_role = "Controller"`.

Stage 1 SHALL additionally provide:

- `notice_event_log_url` **(profile-required)**
- `privacy_rights_controls` **(profile-required)**

#### 6.3.4 Event section (Stage 1)

Stage 1 SHALL include:

- `event_time`
- `event_type` = `notice_issued` (or equivalent notice issuance event)
- `entity_id` (CIR-ID for controller-initiated events)
- `notice_event_log_id` **(profile-required)**

### 6.4 Conformance note

This Stage 1 specification is a **privacy-enabling** notice-first profile: it enables the individual to evaluate the controller and transparency terms prior to any identification or permission enforcement.

## Optional Extensions for Consent Portability and Agentic Coordination

### Section 12: Authorization Exchange Overview

**12.1 Addressing Base Standard Gap**

ISO/IEC 27560:2025 does not specify exchange protocol for privacy receipts. This authorization exchange protocol defines:

- Exchange pattern: How receipts flow between parties across four stages
- Exchange semantics: What each stage authorizes
- Exchange structure: Field additions per stage
- Universal applicability: Same pattern across privacy, safety, security, environment contexts

**12.2 Four-Stage Progression**

**ANCR Exchange Stage 1 (Notice Receipt)**: Notice acknowledgment (bilateral proof)

**ANCR Exchange Stage 2 (Consent Notice Receipt)**: Explicit authorization (consent, contract, policy acceptance, etc.)

**ANCR Exchange Stage 3 (Micro Notice Credential)**: Technical context authorization (API, device, remote verification)

**ANCR Exchange Stage 4 (Consent Token)**: Individual-controlled authorization portability (agentic coordination, consent authorization in digital wallets)

### Section 17: Token Security Considerations

**17.1 Replay Attack Prevention**

- Unique token identifiers (jti)
- Time-bound tokens with expiration
- Nonce-based challenge-response

**17.2 Authorization Freshness Validation**

- Token issuance must be after latest Notice Event Log material change
- Controllers reject tokens issued before policy updates

**17.2.1 Active State Synchronic Validation (Level 4)**

For TATA Level 4 (Active State + Physical Verification) implementations, consent tokens SHALL include cryptographic binding to the notice.txt (CIR) state at time of issuance:

**Synchronic Matching Requirement**:

- Consent tokens SHALL include cryptographic hash of notice.txt (CIR) at time of token issuance
- Token verification SHALL confirm that current notice.txt matches the hashed version included in token
- If controller updates notice.txt (material change to CIR), all previously issued tokens become invalid until individual re-authorizes against updated CIR
- Notice Event Log SHALL record CIR version hash with each token issuance event

**Security Benefits**:

1. **Prevents Stale Consent Exploits**: Tokens automatically invalidate when CIR changes, preventing controllers from relying on old authorizations after changing processing terms
2. **Prevents CIR Hijacking**: Attacker cannot serve fake notice.txt at unauthorized endpoint‚Äîtoken verification fails if CIR hash doesn't match registry-signed version
3. **Prevents Fake Hash Injection**: Token hash must match both current CIR AND Notice Event Log entry‚Äîcontroller cannot forge hash without registry detection
4. **Enables Real-Time Compliance**: Regulators verify token validity by comparing CIR hash against Notice Event Log without controller cooperation

**Implementation Pattern**:

```json
{
  "token_claims": {
    "iss": "CIR-CA-12345",
    "jti": "receipt-abc-123",
    "iat": "2025-12-13T19:00:00-05:00",
    "exp": "2026-12-13T19:00:00-05:00",
    "cir_hash_at_issuance": "sha256:a1b2c3d4...",
    "cir_publication_url": "https://example.com/.well-known/transparency/notice.txt",
    "notice_event_log_verification_url": "https://example.com/.well-known/transparency/notice-event-log.jsonl"
  }
}
```

**Verification Protocol**:

1. Extract `cir_hash_at_issuance` from token
2. Fetch current notice.txt from `cir_publication_url`
3. Compute hash of current notice.txt
4. Compare computed hash with `cir_hash_at_issuance`
5. If mismatch: Token invalid‚ÄîCIR has been updated since token issuance
6. Query Notice Event Log to confirm `cir_hash_at_issuance` matches log entry at token `iat` timestamp
7. If log mismatch: Token invalid‚Äîhash was forged or CIR was hijacked

**Attack Prevention**:

**Attack 1: CIR Hijacking at Unauthorized Endpoint**

- Attacker serves fake notice.txt at compromised endpoint
- Token contains hash of legitimate CIR from registry
- Verification fails: fake notice.txt hash ‚â† token `cir_hash_at_issuance`
- **Defense**: Cryptographic binding prevents acceptance of hijacked CIR

**Attack 2: Fake Hash Injection in Token**

- Attacker creates token with fake `cir_hash_at_issuance`
- Verification queries Notice Event Log for CIR hash at token `iat` timestamp
- Log hash ‚â† token hash (registry signed log cannot be forged)
- **Defense**: Notice Event Log provides authoritative hash history‚Äîtoken rejected if hash doesn't match log

**Attack 3: Stale Consent After Material Change**

- Controller updates notice.txt (changes `scope_of_disclosure` from regional to international)
- Old tokens contain hash of pre-change CIR
- Verification computes hash of current notice.txt
- Current hash ‚â† token `cir_hash_at_issuance`
- **Defense**: Automatic token invalidation forces re-authorization under new terms

**Regulatory Oversight**:

- Supervisory authorities verify token validity by:
    1. Retrieving token from data subject or controller
    2. Querying public Notice Event Log for CIR hash at token issuance time
    3. Comparing with current CIR hash
    4. Automated detection of tokens issued under outdated terms

> **Editorial Note for v1.1**: Active State Synchronic Validation will be standardized as mandatory for TATA Level 4 implementations in v1.1. Current v1.0 version documents the pattern as INFORMATIVE guidance for Level 4 implementers. V1.1 will specify: normative hash algorithm requirements (SHA-256 minimum), Notice Event Log CIR version tracking protocols, and cross-registry CIR hash verification standards for international token portability.

### Appendix E: Open Questions for Reviewers

#### Comments for Reviewers: Article 80 and Article 88 References

**Operational Personal Data Logging and Access Rights**

This profile references **EU Regulation 2018/1725 Articles 80 and 88** as the operational implementation of Convention 108+ transparency requirements for **operational personal data** contexts.

**Article 80 (Right of Access by the Data Subject):**

- Data subjects have the right to access their operational personal data and related information
- Access may be restricted to protect investigations, public security, or rights of others
- Implemented via the universal `rights_access_point` field in the CIR

**Article 88 (Logging):**

- Controllers must keep logs for processing operations in automated processing systems: collection, alteration, access, consultation, disclosure (including transfers), combination, and erasure
- Logs must establish justification, date/time, identification of who consulted/disclosed data, and recipient identity
- Logs available to DPO/EDPS on request
- Implemented via the **Notice Event Log** specification

**Distinction from Lawful Access/Surveillance**:

- Operational personal data (Articles 80/88) = processing by EU institutions for security/law enforcement tasks
- Lawful access/interception = surveillance powers disclosed via `surveillance_risks`

**Reviewer Consideration:**

Does the Notice Event Log specification adequately address Article 88 logging requirements for operational personal data contexts while remaining applicable across all universal contexts (privacy, safety, security, environment)?

#### Comments for Reviewers: PII Principal Jurisdiction Field

**New Field Addition: pii_principal_jurisdiction**

Rationale:

1. Risk-proportionate disclosure (surveillance risks differ by jurisdiction)
2. Informed consent for cross-border transfers
3. Jurisdictional rights determination
4. Adequacy assessment requires source and destination jurisdictions

Privacy-preserving implementation:

- Not collected in Stage 1
- Conditional in Stage 2+ (cross-border contexts)
- Determination method disclosure
- Accuracy/correction support

Reviewer considerations:

1. Does conditional collection balance transparency and privacy preservation?
2. Should determination method disclosure be mandatory?
3. Is ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 / ISO 3166-2 granularity appropriate?
4. Should the field be recommended vs optional when transfer_mechanism="consent"?

Alternative approaches considered:

- Generic disclosure only (rejected)
- Controller jurisdiction proxy (rejected)
- Post-hoc determination (rejected)

### Appendix H: Profile Types for ISO/IEC 27560:2025

#### H.1 Overview of Profile Types

**Profile Type 1: Data Protection Privacy Receipt Profile**

- Control model: Controller-centric (Individual-ID first)
- Receipt timing: After individual creates account and provides PII
- Trust model: Individual depends on controller promises
- Architecture: Centralized

**Profile Type 2: Co-Regulated Digital Privacy Profile** (This document)

- Control model: Co-regulated transparency (Controller-ID first)
- Receipt timing: Before any processing/collection begins
- Trust model: Verifiable through cryptographic receipts and public registries
- Architecture: Distributed

**Profile Type 3: Personal Data Control Profile**

- Control model: Individual-controlled (SSI/DID anchored)
- Receipt timing: Individual presents verifiable credentials on demand
- Trust model: Individual controls authorization distribution
- Architecture: Decentralized

#### H.2 Architectural Comparison

| Aspect | Type 1: Data Protection | Type 2: Co-Regulated (This Profile) | Type 3: Personal Control |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

**K.3.3 Context-Specific Rights Exercise**

GPRC enables:

- Automated Rights APIs per context
- Preference-signal-triggered objection in legitimate interest contexts
- Notice-receipt-anchored withdrawal without authentication
- Regulatory verification of rights availability at scale

**K.3.4 72 Digital Transparency Control Contexts**

- 3 Data control vectors √ó 4 TATA levels √ó 6 legal bases = **72 contexts**.
